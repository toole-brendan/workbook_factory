================================================================================
DDG-51 SAM ANALYSIS — METHODOLOGY ADVICE
Which frame should anchor the analysis: procurement-timing or lifecycle-stage?
================================================================================
Written: 2026-07-01

RECOMMENDATION
--------------
Anchor on the PROCUREMENT-TIMING (group-keel, all-rows) frame, and demote the
lifecycle-stage view to a SCOPED DRILL-DOWN. The two are not really competitors
- they are different rungs of the same denominator ladder: one is a headline,
one is a footnote.

  - Procurement Timing = subaward date vs the PIID group's EARLIEST keel /
    LATEST delivery. Covers all rows. Phases: Advance/LLTM ~63%, In-build ~36%,
    Post-delivery ~1%.
  - Lifecycle Stage = subaward date vs the ASSIGNED hull's OWN keel/launch/
    delivery. Exact-hull (A/B) rows only (~35% of $). Stages: Long-lead 87%,
    Construction 5%, Outfit/test 5%, Post-delivery 3% (of the A/B subset).


WHY PROCUREMENT-TIMING IS THE RIGHT ANCHOR
------------------------------------------
1. COVERAGE - it describes 100% of the evidence; lifecycle describes 35%.
   The timing phase is defined for all 5,006 rows and reconciles to the whole
   observed universe (a Checks invariant). The lifecycle stage is BLANK for
   ~65% of the money (the C/D family-level bulk) - it only exists where you
   already have exact-hull evidence. Anchor on lifecycle and you are silently
   analyzing a third of the corpus; every headline is really "...of the
   exact-hull slice."

2. GRAIN DISCIPLINE - timing respects it; lifecycle cannot scale without
   breaking it. The timing phase is a property of the BLOCK's schedule, never a
   hull assignment, so it extends cleanly to the family-level majority. The
   lifecycle stage is welded to a single assigned hull; there is no honest way
   to push it onto C/D rows. The whole model is built on "don't force family
   dollars onto one hull" - timing is the frame that obeys that at 100%
   coverage.

3. ROBUST / CONSERVATIVE. Group-earliest-keel calls a subaward "advance" only
   if it predates the ENTIRE block - so it can't be gamed by which ceremonial
   keel-date you pick, and it handles the grand-block edge case correctly
   (In-build, not Long-lead). The own-keel lifecycle measure over-counts: it
   inflates "long-lead" with future-hull spend and mislabels outsourced
   construction. For a number you defend in front of a skeptical audience, take
   the conservative one.

4. IT CARRIES THE LOAD-BEARING STORY. "~2/3 of supplier evidence is advance
   procurement -> that's why hull attribution caps at ~35%, why observed SAM is
   a supplier-and-timing map, ~10% of the construction contract, ~50% of TAM."
   That entire narrative rides on the broad timing frame. The lifecycle "87%"
   invites exactly the pushback already encountered (grand blocks, own-keel
   ambiguity, only-A/B).


BUT KEEP LIFECYCLE - AS A DRILL-DOWN, CLEARLY SCOPED
---------------------------------------------------
The lifecycle stage is genuinely more informative WHERE IT APPLIES: it is the
only thing that tells you where in a specific ship's build a dollar lands, and
it is where the vendor stories live - fuel at outfit/test (Martin Energy),
trial-card / warranty fixes post-delivery (Southcoast Welding, Naval Engineering
Support), panels/coatings during construction. Use it for the "here's how spend
flows across a hull's life, and who supplies each stage" exhibit - ALWAYS
labeled "of the exact-hull (A/B) subset."


THE DIVISION OF LABOR
---------------------
  Use PROCUREMENT TIMING (anchor) for:
    - coverage / denominator claims
    - the "front-loaded / advance-procurement" headline
    - timing by builder / SWBS
    - anything spanning the whole corpus

  Use LIFECYCLE STAGE (drill-down) for:
    - the exact-hull build-sequence narrative
    - stage-level vendor / work stories
    - scoped to the ~35% you can trace to a hull


THE ONE TRAP TO AVOID
---------------------
Never quote 87% as a program-level figure. It is 87% OF THE 35% exact-hull
slice; the whole-program advance share is 63%. Mixing those is the single
easiest way to get a number wrong by ~1/3.


ONE UPGRADE WORTH MAKING
------------------------
If you want the anchor even cleaner, add a MATERIAL-vs-CONSTRUCTION split to the
procurement-timing frame - separate advance MATERIAL (turbines, gears,
switchgear) from EARLY CONSTRUCTION (the ~$55M grand blocks + structural
pre-fab), which are separable by vendor type and SWBS. That fixes the only real
conceptual seam in the anchor and lets you say "advance material is X, outsourced
construction is Y" instead of lumping them.


BOTTOM LINE
-----------
Lead every whole-program claim with procurement-timing; bring in lifecycle only
when you have explicitly narrowed to the exact-hull ships and want the finer
build-stage / vendor texture. Broad frame anchors; fine frame colors.
